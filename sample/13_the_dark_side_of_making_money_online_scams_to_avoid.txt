The Dark Side of Making Money Online: Scams to Avoid

HOW CAN I SPOT A MAKE-MONEY-ONLINE SCAM BEFORE I LOSE MONEY?

Look for three red flags: upfront fees, guaranteed income, and vague job descriptions. Scammers demand payment for "training," "starter kits," or "exclusive access" before you earn a cent. Legitimate opportunities pay you, not the other way around. Guaranteed income claims like "earn $5,000 a month with no experience" are impossible to promise. Real work requires effort, skill, or time. Vague descriptions like "easy online work" or "secret system" hide the fact there’s no real product or service. Always research the company on the Better Business Bureau, Trustpilot, or Reddit. If you can’t find verifiable reviews or the company has no online presence, walk away.

HOW DO PYRAMID SCHEMES DISGUISE THEMSELVES AS LEGITIMATE BUSINESSES?

Pyramid schemes call themselves "multi-level marketing" (MLM) or "direct sales" but focus on recruiting, not selling real products. You’ll recognize them by their emphasis on building a "downline" (recruiting others) over actual sales. Legitimate MLMs, like Avon or Tupperware, generate most revenue from product sales, not recruitment. Pyramid schemes collapse when recruitment slows, leaving 99% of participants with losses. Check the company’s income disclosure statement—if most participants earn little or nothing, it’s a pyramid. The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has shut down companies like Herbalife and AdvoCare for operating as pyramids. If the pitch sounds like "get rich by bringing in friends," it’s a scam.

WHAT’S THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN AFFILIATE MARKETING AND A PYRAMID SCHEME?

Affiliate marketing pays you to promote real products or services. You earn a commission when someone buys through your unique link. Companies like Amazon, Shopify, and ClickBank run legitimate affiliate programs. Pyramid schemes pay you to recruit others, not sell products. Affiliate marketers succeed by driving traffic and conversions, not by signing up new members. Pyramid schemes rely on endless recruitment, which is unsustainable. If a program requires you to pay to join or buy inventory before earning, it’s a pyramid. Affiliate programs are free to join and don’t force you to stockpile products.

HOW DO "GET-RICH-QUICK" COURSES SCAM PEOPLE?

These courses sell dreams, not skills. They promise "secret formulas" or "proven systems" to make thousands overnight but deliver generic, outdated, or plagiarized content. Scammers use fake testimonials, staged income screenshots, and high-pressure sales tactics to convince you to buy. The course often costs hundreds or thousands, but the "secrets" are basic strategies you can find for free on YouTube or blogs. Some even upsell you into a "mastermind" or "coaching" program for more money. Before buying, search for free alternatives. If the course creator can’t show real, verifiable success stories or a money-back guarantee, it’s a scam.

WHY DO "PAID SURVEY" SITES FAIL TO PAY USERS?

Paid survey sites make money by selling your data or driving traffic to advertisers, not by paying you fairly. They lure you in with promises of $50 per survey but only pay pennies for hours of work. Many sites have payout thresholds you’ll never reach, like $50, while others disappear when you try to cash out. Legitimate survey sites like Swagbucks or InboxDollars exist but pay minimally—think $1 to $5 per hour. If a site asks for payment to join or guarantees high earnings, it’s a scam. Treat surveys as a side hobby, not a real income source.

HOW DO FAKE "REMOTE JOB" OFFERS WORK?

Scammers post fake job listings on Indeed, LinkedIn, or Craigslist for "data entry," "customer service," or "virtual assistant" roles. They interview you via email or chat, then "hire" you quickly. The catch? They send you a fake check to buy "equipment" or "software," then ask you to wire part of the money back. The check bounces, leaving you liable for the full amount. Legitimate remote jobs don’t ask for money upfront or send you checks before work starts. Always verify the company’s website, LinkedIn page, and employee reviews. If the job seems too easy or the pay too high, it’s a scam.

WHAT SHOULD I DO IF I’VE ALREADY FALLEN FOR A SCAM?

Act fast. If you paid with a credit card, dispute the charge with your bank immediately. For wire transfers or cryptocurrency, report it to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov. Gather all evidence—emails, receipts, screenshots—and file a police report. If the scam involved a fake job, report it to the job site where you found it. Warn others by leaving reviews on Trustpilot or the Better Business Bureau. Scammers often target the same victims with "recovery scams," promising to get your money back for a fee. Never pay to recover lost funds. The sooner you act, the better your chances of limiting the damage.
